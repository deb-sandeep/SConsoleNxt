## 4. Actionable insights

Insights are grouped by lap in the order L1, L2P, L2, AMR, L3P, L3.1, L3.2. Each lap section has three subsections: what went right, what went wrong, and betterment.

### L1

**What went right.**

- The first-pass reading of questions was accurate. Most questions were classified correctly within the first ten seconds. The right ones were marked for L1 conversion. The right ones were deferred. The first decision point worked on most of the paper.
- The hard questions were recognised quickly. Q22, Q23, Q33, Q40 and Q44 were read briefly and deferred without hesitation. The instinct for "this is not an L1 question" was sharp.

**What went wrong.**

- Two questions were committed in under 30 seconds. Q20 (24s) and Q25 (23s). Both ended correct, but the L1 commit window is one to two minutes for a reason. A sub-30-second commit means the answer was selected on instinct alone. The verification step that the L1 window provides was skipped. The marks came in this time. The next time the same instinct fires on a question that looks similar but is not, the wrong answer will be locked in just as fast.
- Two questions were never engaged. Q19 (States of Matter) and Q33 (Trigonometry). Both received brief L1 sweep time (33s and 15s respectively) but were never explicitly returned to. Both ended NOT_VISITED. Both were tagged STUPID in the self-eval. Both lost four marks each. Eight marks gone before the difficulty of the questions ever entered the picture.
- The first decision point misfired on five questions. Q5, Q6, Q10, Q13 and Q39. Q6 became wrong. The others recovered, but only because L2P and L2 caught what L1 had misclassified. The other laps absorbed the cost.

**Betterment.**

- Honour the full one-to-two-minute L1 conversion window. When the decision is "convert in L1", the work is the work. Selecting an option in 25 seconds is not L1 conversion; it is intuition with a stopwatch. The discipline is to read the options, do the substitution or the calculation that the question asks for, and only then commit.
- Every question must be engaged at least to the first decision point. The L1 sweep can only correctly defer a question if it has actually been read. Q19 and Q33 had L1 time but never reached the convert-or-defer decision in earnest. The discipline is to ensure every question receives a deliberate first-decision call before the sweep moves on.
- When the first decision point feels uncertain, defer rather than convert. The first decision point carries higher consequence than the second. Getting "convert or defer" wrong is more expensive than getting "L2 or L3" wrong. If the call at ten seconds is not clear, the safer move is to defer and let L2P do the strategising work.

### L2P

**What went right.**

- L2P was used on 26 questions. Nineteen of these closed cleanly with PERFECT_EXECUTION. The lap is not broken at the structural level. It works on most questions.
- Q41 is the strongest L2P recovery case. The L2P showed INSUFFICIENT_ANALYSIS but the student kept going through L2 and got it right. Resilience after a weak L2P is real and worth naming.

**What went wrong.**

- L2P planning was treated as a gate to clear, not as the actual work. Six questions show INSUFFICIENT_ANALYSIS at L2P with self-eval scores of 2 or 3. Four of these six cascaded into lossy outcomes. This is the single largest avoidable cost in the attempt.
- The notes are explicit about the mechanism. Q11 L2P: "Comprehension was sorted. Strategy was half-baked." Q28 L2P: "Strategy not fully baked. Did not consider all the values given." Q31 L2P: "Analysis should be concrete. Its not about gut feel of 'it will happen'." Q42 L2P: "Eager to end L2P so thought will solve it in L2."
- The Q24 note is the diagnostic case. "Made an error in strategy here which I was forced to discover in L2. Costed time." The strategy error was discoverable in L2P. It was not discovered. L2 then absorbed the cost.

**Betterment.**

- Add a one-sentence strategy assertion at L2P closure. Before leaving L2P, the strategy must be statable in one sentence. "I will set up the equation in x, eliminate y using condition Z, solve." If this sentence will not form, the question is not L2-ready. This is a procedural change. It does not require thinking harder. It forces an explicit checkpoint at the boundary.
- The "eager to end L2P" posture is the target to break. The Q42 note names it. It will recur. Catching it is half the fix.

### L2

**What went right.**

- Q4 recovered from an L2 abort-failure to end correct. Laws of Motion, 314s in L2, tagged ABORT_FAILURE plus MORE_TIME_SPENT. The student did not give up after the abort. She rebuilt the strategy and closed the question.
- Q24 was a long L2 grind that ended correct. Atomic Structure, 288s in L2, tagged MORE_TIME_SPENT. The L2P had carried an INSUFFICIENT_ANALYSIS, which the L2 had to absorb. The student stayed with it and got the answer. Different mechanism from Q4 but the same outcome: persistence under pressure.
- Q41 is the most striking salvage. 482 seconds in L2 on a Complex Numbers question. The L2P had been weak. She ground through and got it right. This is genuine resilience under load.

**What went wrong.**

- Three questions were held in L2 without converging. Q11 (263s), Q21 (315s), Q31 (338s). Each one became a sink. The strategy did not converge. The persistence cost time that other questions needed. None of the three was answered in the end.
- The signal to abort came and was not acted on. The Q31 note says it directly: "Strategy did not converge and was feeling resistance. Should have aborted." The recognition was real time. The decision was not.
- One L2 commit went wrong because L2P had been rushed. Q42. The L2P note: "Eager to end L2P so thought will solve it in L2." L2 then committed to an answer with full confidence. The answer was wrong. L2 cannot fix what L2P did not finish.

**Betterment.**

- Set an abort trigger and act on it. Ninety seconds into L2 without measurable progress, abort. Measurable progress means a concrete piece of the solution: a partial answer, an expression that simplifies, a substitution that reveals structure. "Still thinking" is not progress. The trigger removes the judgment from the moment of decision and puts it where it belongs: before the moment.
- L2 is not a recovery lap for incomplete L2P. When L2P closes without a clear strategy, the question is not L2-ready. Pushing it into L2 to "figure it out there" is the move that produced Q42. The fix is upstream, in L2P, not in L2.

### AMR

**What went right.**

- AMR was meaningfully used on three questions. Q8 (Laws of Motion, 46s, CORRECT), Q26 (Atomic Structure, 32s, CORRECT) and Q29 (Atomic Structure, 62s, INCORRECT). Three other questions show one-second AMR touches; these are noise, not actual AMR usage.
- The decision to keep AMR brief overall was correct. AMR is optional by design. Two minutes total is fine when used selectively.

**What went wrong.**

- Q29 was answered in AMR and ended INCORRECT. The note records no specific lapse. The risk of AMR is committing on the rebound rather than verifying carefully. This is one instance, not a pattern.

**Betterment.**

- Reroute AMR to verify L1 fast commits. Q20 and Q25 were committed in 23-24 seconds at L1. They were correct this time. The L1 BAD_DECISION pattern says fast commits carry tail risk. A 30-second AMR check on each fast L1 commit is cheap insurance against future misclassifications that the speed of commit will hide.

### L3P

**What went right.**

- Nine questions received short L3P reads and were correctly set aside without further pursuit. Q3, Q11, Q19, Q21, Q22, Q23, Q27, Q28 and Q31 all show L3P durations between one and sixteen seconds, all tagged PERFECT_EXECUTION. The "no regret" judgment was honest. These were genuinely lost causes given remaining time and information.
- Q22 is the cleanest example. Pressure was real. Other questions were pending. The decision to leave it was still correct. Mature judgment under load.

**What went wrong.**

- L3P was rushed on the questions it should have planned carefully. Five questions show shortfall tags at L3P. Q40, Q43 and Q44 carry both LESS_TIME_SPENT and INSUFFICIENT_ANALYSIS. Q32 carries INSUFFICIENT_ANALYSIS without a time complaint, which is a different failure: time was spent but not on planning. Q45 carries LESS_TIME_SPENT, indicating the lap was sprinted past. The L3P pass that should have triaged carefully and strategised was instead a sprint.
- The consequence shows in L3.1. Q43 went into L3.1 with the L3P note: "Rushed by less remaining time and quite a few unattempted question. Panic had set in." The panic state did not start in L3.1. It started in L3P and got carried forward.
- Q44 carries PRESSURE_ABORT in L3P itself. The pressure had reached the lap that exists to manage pressure.

**Betterment.**

- L3P needs a guaranteed minimum duration. Five minutes was used this attempt. Eight to ten minutes is a more realistic floor for a paper with this many MFR and unanswered questions. Earlier laps need to release the time. The discipline at L1, L2P and L2 is what funds L3P.
- L3P is a strategising lap as much as a triage lap. Routing to L3.1 is half the job. Naming the strategy for each L3.1 question is the other half. The current L3P shows triage but not strategising. The L3.1 outcomes reflect this.

### L3.1

**What went right.**

- Q39 closed in L3.1. Theory of Equations, CORRECT, 106 seconds in L3.1 with PERFECT_EXECUTION. This is the canonical L3.1 success. A question that did not yield in L1 was correctly routed via L3P to L3.1. The reduced pressure environment let her crack it. The lap structure worked end to end on this question.

**What went wrong.**

- Two calculation slips in L3.1 were panic-driven, not knowledge-driven. Q43 (Complex Numbers, INCORRECT, RECOLLECTION) and Q45 (Complex Numbers, INCORRECT, CALCULATION). Both topics she knows. Both questions were within reach. The L1 PERFECT_EXECUTION tags on both confirm she read them clearly.
- The Q43 note is explicit. "Panic had set in." The note appears at L3P, before L3.1 began. The panic state corrupted the L3.1 execution. The question was completed. The answer was wrong.
- Q32 burned 240 seconds in L3.1 without converging. Significant time on a single stuck question. The wrestling did not reach an answer. The time was effectively lost.

**Betterment.**

- L3.1 needs a per-question soft cap. Ninety seconds without progress, force a re-triage. Three options at the boundary: commit a guess, abort, or continue with explicit acknowledgment that more time is being spent. Q32 spent 240 seconds without this discipline. The cap protects the rest of the L3.1 backlog from a single sink.
- Recognise the panic state as a stop-condition. When the note can fairly read "panic had set in", the better call is to commit a marked guess and move on. A panicked calculation is more likely wrong than right. The data on Q43 and Q45 supports this directly.
